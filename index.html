In August 2025, I ran an experiment in South Florida to test whether cutting the tip of milkweed seeds (a process called scarification) would help them germinate faster than seeds left untouched. I tested this method on both swamp milkweed (Asclepias incarnata) and butterfly weed (Asclepias tuberosa).

  • Swamp milkweed and butterfly weed seeds (not cold-stratified)
  • Clean cups filled with water
  • Scissors (to scarify seeds)
  • Float container (optional)
  • Shaded outdoor area (South Florida climate)

I cut the tip off half of the seeds and placed both groups (scarified and non-scarified) in cups of water, then floated them outdoors in the shade. The temperature stayed warm and humid throughout.

ğŸ”¬ Why This Works

Scarification helps water and oxygen get past the seed coat and reach the embryo faster. In a hot and humid climate like South Florida, this triggers quick germination even without cold stratification. Most guides recommend cold stratifying milkweed seeds for 30 days, but this experiment shows it's not always necessary!
